WHY CLEARANCES MATTER
⚠️
If a traction elevator overspeeds in the UP direction and the counterweight contacts its buffer, the only thing protecting the person on top of the car is correct clearance. If those measurements are wrong — someone gets hurt or killed.
⚙️ How Traction Safety Works

When the counterweight lands on its buffer, its weight is removed from the rope system. This causes the drive sheave to lose traction — the ropes slip — and the car stops moving up. This is a passive, built-in safety feature that works only if clearances are correct.

⚡
A worn or corrugated sheave can have enough traction to pull the car into the overhead even after CWT buffer contact. This is why sheave condition matters.
📐 Three Areas You Must Check
1
Counterweight Runby — Distance CWT travels before hitting its buffer. Must be within code limits.
2
Top of Car Clearance — Space above crosshead and refuge for a person on the car top when CWT hits its buffer.
3
Pit Clearance — Space beneath the car when it rests on fully compressed buffers. Protects anyone in the pit.

COUNTERWEIGHT RUNBY
📏 What Is Runby?

Runby is the gap between the bottom of the counterweight frame and the top of the counterweight buffer — measured when the car is at the top terminal landing. It's how far the CWT can travel before hitting its buffer.

There is also a top runby — measured when the car is at the bottom landing — which is the gap between the top of the CWT and the overhead structure.

```
📋
A CWT Runby Data Plate is required in the pit showing the designed runby. Missing plate = code violation.
🔧 How to Measure — Step by Step
1
Position the car at the top terminal landing — car sill level with the top floor landing sill.
2
Go to the pit. Locate the counterweight buffer and the bottom of the CWT frame directly above it.
3
Measure the gap from the top of the CWT buffer to the bottom of the CWT frame. That is your bottom CWT runby.
4
Repeat with the car at the bottom terminal landing for the top CWT runby.
5
Compare your measurements to the data plate and code minimums. Flag any discrepancy.
✅
Note: In some installs the buffer is slightly pre-compressed (up to 25% of stroke) when the car is at the top landing — this is allowed by code and means bottom runby may be zero.
```

TOP OF CAR CLEARANCE
🧮 The MUM Formula — Maximum Upward Movement

MUM is the total distance the car could travel above the top landing after the CWT hits its buffer. Every top-of-car clearance must exceed MUM by a code-required margin.

MUM = CWT Runby + Buffer Stroke + Jump
CWT Runby = measured gap, bottom of CWT frame to top of CWT buffer
Buffer Stroke = full travel distance of the CWT buffer
Jump = ½ × (V² ÷ 19,320) using V = 115% of rated speed in FPM
📌
The 115% speed accounts for the window between actual speed and governor overspeed trip point. This ensures worst-case protection.

🔧 Field Measurement Steps
1
Level car sill with the top terminal landing floor. This is your reference position for all measurements.
2
Measure crosshead clearance: From the top of the crosshead up to the lowest overhead structure (beam, sheave, machine, etc.).
3
Measure refuge space: From the car top platform up to the lowest overhead point. This must accommodate a standing person.
4
Measure any equipment projecting above the crosshead (conduit heads, switches, etc.) to the overhead.
5
Calculate MUM for this elevator. Compare each measurement against MUM + the required add-on. All must pass.

PIT CLEARANCE
⬇️ What We're Protecting Against

If someone is in the pit when the car descends to its buffer, there must be enough space under the car to keep them safe. The code requires a refuge space — a clear zone a person could crouch in and survive.

Measurements are taken with the car resting on fully compressed buffers — the worst-case position.

📏 Code Minimums (ASME A17.1)
24"
Min vertical clearance — pit floor to lowest car part

Exceptions: Guide shoes, safety jaw assemblies, and platform aprons/guards located within 12 inches horizontally from the sides of the car platform are excluded from this measurement.

🚫
No part of the car or any attached equipment shall contact the pit floor, pit equipment, or any pit structure when the car is on fully compressed buffers.
🔧 How to Measure — Step by Step
1
Calculate the compressed buffer position. Lower the car until the buffer is fully compressed, or use pit depth minus full buffer stroke to find where the car would end up.
2
Identify the lowest point on the car underframe — excluding guide shoes and safety jaws within 12" of platform sides.
3
Measure from pit floor up to that lowest structural/mechanical point. Must be ≥ 24 inches.
4
Verify pit equipment clearance. Check that buffers, conduit, junction boxes, and any pit-mounted gear won't be contacted by the car at full compression.
5
Verify pit depth is sufficient for buffer stroke + required clearance. A too-shallow pit may not allow full buffer compression within code limits.
✅
Always check pit for condition issues too — standing water, missing stop switch, inadequate lighting. All affect safe maintenance access.
